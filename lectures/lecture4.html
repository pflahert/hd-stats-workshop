Lecture 4: Seventy Genes and a Fraud

Penalized Regression, Validation, and the Limits of a Method

Published

Invalid Date

Perfect training, catastrophic prediction

Consider a research team fitting ordinary least squares to predict patient outcomes from 500 gene-expression features measured on 80 patients. The training \(R^2\) is 0.99: the model explains the data almost perfectly. The test \(R^2\), evaluated on a held-out cohort, is \(-0.3\) — worse than predicting the mean for everyone.

The model has memorized noise. With more features than samples, the OLS system is underdetermined: there are infinitely many coefficient vectors that interpolate the training data exactly. Among these, the one that the standard pseudoinverse selects has no particular reason to generalize, and in practice it almost never does. The training fit reflects the degeneracy of the system, not any property of the underlying biology.

This is the canonical failure of unregularized regression in the high-dimensional regime. The remedy — adding a penalty term that prefers some solutions over others — is the subject of this lecture. The lecture develops the three principal regularizers (ridge, LASSO, elastic net), examines the geometry that produces sparse solutions, and then turns to two case studies in which the same statistical method, applied to the same kind of data for the same kind of question, produced dramatically different outcomes. The difference, as will become clear, has nothing to do with the choice of penalty.

The regression problem in high dimensions

The linear model \(y = X\beta + \varepsilon\) with \(\varepsilon \sim N(0, \sigma^2 I)\) admits the OLS solution \(\hat\beta = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y\) when \(X^\top X\) is invertible. When \(p > n\), \(X^\top X\) has rank at most \(n < p\) and is therefore singular: \((X^\top X)^{-1}\) does not exist. Even when \(p < n\) but \(p\) is comparable to \(n\), OLS overfits — its variance explodes, and prediction degrades on new data.

The regularization strategy adds a penalty term to the objective that constrains the coefficients in some way. The general form is

\[\hat\beta_{\mathrm{pen}} = \arg\min_\beta \left\{ \frac{1}{2n}\|y - X\beta\|_2^2 + \lambda \cdot \mathrm{pen}(\beta) \right\},\]

where the penalty \(\mathrm{pen}(\beta)\) is a function of the coefficient vector and \(\lambda \geq 0\) controls the strength of the regularization. The exchange is precise: regularization introduces bias into the estimates in return for reduced variance. The optimal \(\lambda\) — the one that minimizes prediction error — balances the two.

Three penalties dominate practice. The \(\ell_2\) penalty \(\frac{1}{2}\|\beta\|_2^2\) produces ridge regression. The \(\ell_1\) penalty \(\|\beta\|_1\) produces the LASSO. A convex combination of the two produces the elastic net. The differences among them have substantive statistical consequences.

Ridge regression

Ridge regression solves

\[\hat\beta_{\mathrm{ridge}} = \arg\min_\beta \left\{ \frac{1}{2n}\|y - X\beta\|_2^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2}\|\beta\|_2^2 \right\}.\]

The closed-form solution is \(\hat\beta_{\mathrm{ridge}} = (X^\top X + n\lambda I)^{-1}X^\top y\). The penalty term renders the system invertible even when \(p > n\): \(X^\top X + n\lambda I\) is positive definite for any \(\lambda > 0\).

Ridge has two distinguishing properties. It shrinks all coefficients toward zero, but none of them exactly to zero — every variable retains a (small) nonzero coefficient. And it admits an exact Bayesian interpretation: the ridge solution is the posterior mode under the prior \(\beta_j \sim N(0, \tau^2)\), with \(\lambda = \sigma^2/\tau^2\). Larger \(\lambda\) corresponds to a tighter prior on small coefficients.

The bias–variance tradeoff is direct. As \(\lambda\) increases, bias rises (coefficients shrink away from their unbiased OLS values) and variance falls (estimates become more stable across samples). The mean squared error of \(\hat\beta_{\mathrm{ridge}}\) decomposes as

\[\mathrm{MSE}(\hat\beta_{\mathrm{ridge}}) = \mathrm{Bias}^2 + \mathrm{Variance},\]

and an optimal \(\lambda\) minimizes the sum. Ridge is the appropriate tool when the underlying coefficient vector is dense — when many predictors have small effects rather than a few having large ones.

A geometric account of sparsity

The defining feature of LASSO, in contrast to ridge, is that it produces solutions in which many coefficients are exactly zero. The mechanism is geometric.

The constrained form of LASSO is \(\min \|y - X\beta\|^2\) subject to \(\|\beta\|_1 \leq t\). The constraint set \(\{\beta : \|\beta\|_1 \leq t\}\) is a cross-polytope (a “diamond”) in \(\mathbb{R}^p\) whose vertices lie on the coordinate axes. The corresponding ridge constraint set is the Euclidean ball — smooth, with no distinguished points.

The LASSO solution is the point of tangency between the level sets of the squared-error loss and the constraint set. The level sets are concentric ellipsoids centered at the OLS solution. As they expand outward, they generically first contact the diamond at a vertex — a point where one or more coordinates are exactly zero. They generically contact the ball at an arbitrary point on its surface, where all coordinates are typically nonzero. Sparsity is therefore not a tuning artifact of LASSO but a geometric consequence of the \(\ell_1\) ball’s shape.

In \(p\) dimensions, the \(\ell_1\) ball has \(2p\) vertices, one on each signed axis. The probability of vertex contact grows with dimension, and so does the typical sparsity of the LASSO solution. This effect — sparsity emerging from high-dimensional geometry — is one of the reasons the method scales so well.

The LASSO

The LASSO (Tibshirani, 1996) solves

\[\hat\beta_{\mathrm{lasso}} = \arg\min_\beta \left\{ \frac{1}{2n}\|y - X\beta\|_2^2 + \lambda\|\beta\|_1 \right\}.\]

The \(\ell_1\) penalty performs estimation and variable selection simultaneously: the optimization yields a coefficient vector with most entries set to zero, and the nonzero entries identify the variables retained in the model.

The regularization path traces \(\hat\beta(\lambda)\) as \(\lambda\) varies from \(\lambda_{\max}\) (where all coefficients are zero) to zero (where the LASSO approaches the OLS solution, when it exists). The path is piecewise linear in \(\lambda\), a property exploited by the LARS algorithm (Efron, Hastie, Johnstone, and Tibshirani, 2004) to compute the entire solution path efficiently. Variables enter the active set one at a time as \(\lambda\) decreases, providing a natural ordering by importance.

Choosing \(\lambda\) by cross-validation

The regularization strength \(\lambda\) is chosen by \(K\)-fold cross-validation. The data are split into \(K\) folds (typically \(K = 10\)). For each candidate value of \(\lambda\), the model is fit on \(K - 1\) folds and evaluated on the held-out fold; the cross-validation error is averaged across folds. The optimal \(\lambda\) minimizes the CV error.

Two specific choices recur in practice. The value \(\lambda_{\min}\) minimizes the CV error and produces the model with the best out-of-sample prediction. The value \(\lambda_{1\mathrm{se}}\) is the largest \(\lambda\) within one standard error of the minimum; it produces a more parsimonious model with prediction error statistically indistinguishable from \(\lambda_{\min}\). For interpretive applications — gene signatures meant to be reported in publications or assembled into clinical assays — \(\lambda_{1\mathrm{se}}\) is generally preferable. A 12-gene signature is more interpretable, more likely to replicate, and more practical to deploy than a 45-gene one with comparable accuracy.

Elastic net

The LASSO has a known weakness in correlated-predictor settings. When two or more variables are highly correlated, the LASSO tends to select one and drop the others arbitrarily; the selection is unstable across resamples even when the predictive performance is essentially unchanged. In genomics this is the rule rather than the exception: co-regulated genes vary together, and correlated transcripts compete for inclusion in any sparse signature.

The elastic net (Zou and Hastie, 2005) addresses this by combining the \(\ell_1\) and \(\ell_2\) penalties:

\[\hat\beta_{\mathrm{enet}} = \arg\min_\beta \left\{ \frac{1}{2n}\|y - X\beta\|_2^2 + \lambda\!\left[\alpha\|\beta\|_1 + \frac{1-\alpha}{2}\|\beta\|_2^2\right] \right\}.\]

The mixing parameter \(\alpha \in [0,1]\) interpolates between ridge (\(\alpha = 0\)) and LASSO (\(\alpha = 1\)). When \(\alpha\) is strictly between zero and one, the elastic net retains the variable-selection property of the LASSO while exhibiting the grouping effect: highly correlated predictors tend to be selected or omitted as a group.

Theoretical guarantees

Two classes of guarantees clarify when and how well the LASSO works. Under the irrepresentable condition and an appropriate signal-to-noise level, Zhao and Yu (2006) showed that the LASSO recovers the true support — the set of nonzero coefficients — with probability tending to one as \(n \to \infty\). The condition restricts how strongly correlated the truly null variables can be with the truly non-null ones; when it fails, exact support recovery is generally impossible by LASSO alone.

Under the weaker restricted eigenvalue conditions, the LASSO achieves prediction error of order

\[\frac{1}{n}\|X\hat\beta_{\mathrm{lasso}} - X\beta^*\|_2^2 = O\!\left(\frac{s \log p}{n}\right),\]

where \(s\) is the number of true nonzero coefficients. This is near-optimal: the price paid for sparsity in dimension \(p\) is only a logarithmic factor, not a polynomial one. The scaling is the formal expression of the intuition that, when the signal is sparse, prediction is feasible even in regimes where \(p\) vastly exceeds \(n\).

Two case studies: validation determines outcome

The same statistical methods, applied to the same kind of data for the same kind of question, can produce dramatically different outcomes. Two case studies make the point.

In 2002, van ’t Veer and colleagues measured 25,000 genes on 78 breast-cancer patients and identified a 70-gene signature predicting metastasis. The signature was commercialized as MammaPrint, cleared by the United States Food and Drug Administration, and prospectively validated in the MINDACT trial (Cardoso et al., 2016). MINDACT randomized 6,693 patients with high clinical but low genomic risk to chemotherapy or no chemotherapy. The five-year distant-metastasis-free survival rate without chemotherapy was 94.7 percent. Approximately 46 percent of women who would have received chemotherapy on the basis of clinical risk alone were spared it on the basis of the genomic test.

At Duke University, Anil Potti and colleagues used analogous gene-expression signatures to predict tumor response to specific chemotherapy drugs. Clinical trials enrolled patients on the basis of the signatures. Two statisticians at MD Anderson — Keith Baggerly and Kevin Coombes — spent several years attempting to reproduce the analyses (Baggerly and Coombes, 2009). They identified mislabeled samples, off-by-one errors in gene indices, and conclusions that were the inverse of what the data actually supported. The episode resulted in eighteen retracted papers and the revocation of Potti’s medical license. Patients had been assigned to treatments on the basis of erroneous models.

The two cases used the same class of statistical methods on the same kind of high-dimensional biological data for the same kind of clinical question. They produced opposite outcomes. The differences had nothing to do with the choice of penalty or the cross-validation scheme. They concerned the analysis pipeline:

  • Validation. MammaPrint was tested in a 6,693-patient prospective randomized trial. The Duke signatures were not subjected to comparable external validation.
  • Reproducibility. The Duke analysis could not be reproduced because code and data were not openly shared. The MammaPrint signature was independently replicated.
  • Information leakage. The Duke pipeline allowed information from the test set to influence training. The MammaPrint pipeline maintained strict separation.

The lesson is that regularization is a tool, not a guarantee. The method is only as reliable as the analysis pipeline that surrounds it, and the pipeline must include external validation, open code and data, and rigorous training/test separation.

A reproducibility caveat: LASSO selection instability

Even when the analysis pipeline is correct, LASSO selection has an inherent instability. When predictors are correlated — as gene-expression measurements typically are — the LASSO selects one representative from each correlated group, with the choice determined essentially arbitrarily by sampling noise. Resampling the data produces different selected subsets while leaving prediction accuracy nearly unchanged. The reported gene list, in such cases, is more a function of the particular sample than of the underlying biology.

Bootstrap stability analysis is the standard remedy. The procedure resamples the dataset many times, refits the LASSO on each resample, and records the frequency with which each variable is selected. Variables that appear in the selected set in a high fraction of resamples (typically more than 50 percent) constitute the stable signature; variables that appear sporadically are unstable and should not be reported as confident discoveries. The elastic net mitigates the instability via its grouping property, but does not eliminate it.

The interpretive lesson is that “the LASSO selected gene \(X\)” is a statement about a particular fit, not about the underlying biology. A defensible signature must survive resampling.

Generative AI in high-dimensional analysis

Large language models have changed the day-to-day practice of statistical analysis. They generate code competently, explain output, suggest analysis strategies, and produce first-pass biological interpretations. When used carefully, they accelerate routine work substantially.

Their failure modes are distinctive. Generated code can contain subtle bugs that produce plausible but wrong output: a missing scale.=TRUE in a PCA call, a forgotten p-value adjustment, an off-by-one error in indexing. Reported biological functions can be fabricated: gene names invented, citations hallucinated, mechanisms confused. The errors are not random; they cluster in domains where the model’s training data are weakest or where the surface form of an answer is similar to a correct answer.

A common-errors checklist for code review is helpful:

  • Was multiple-testing correction applied? Look for p.adjust() or its equivalent.
  • If the data are converted to \(z\)-scores, was the empirical null checked? A wide central peak in the \(z\)-score histogram indicates that \(f_0 \neq N(0,1)\).
  • Is t-SNE run on the raw \(p\)-dimensional data, or on the top principal components? The former is slower and noisier.
  • Was scale.=TRUE set in the PCA call? Variables on different scales otherwise dominate the leading components.
  • Was \(\lambda_{\min}\) or \(\lambda_{1\mathrm{se}}\) used, and is the choice justified?

For biological interpretations: pick at least three genes from any AI-generated functional summary, look them up in GeneCards or NCBI Gene, and verify that the functions and citations are real.

Synthesis

The complete high-dimensional analysis toolkit, as developed across the workshop, comprises five components:

  1. Multiple testing and FDR. When testing thousands of hypotheses, the false discovery rate is the right inferential target, and Benjamini–Hochberg or Storey’s \(q\)-values provide the operational tools.
  2. Empirical Bayes. When \(m\) is large, the aggregate of tests carries information about each individual one; the two-groups model formalizes this idea, and the local FDR is the most informative per-test summary.
  3. Dimension reduction. PCA is the optimal linear method, and is the appropriate first step. Nonlinear methods (t-SNE, UMAP) reveal additional structure but distort distances and cluster sizes; they are tools for exploration.
  4. Penalized regression. Ridge, LASSO, and elastic net handle the \(p > n\) regression problem through regularization. The choice of penalty depends on the structure of the signal: dense (ridge), sparse (LASSO), or sparse with correlated predictors (elastic net).
  5. AI as a tool. Generative models accelerate routine analysis, but every statistical claim and biological interpretation requires verification.

The thread that runs through all five components is that high-dimensional inference is a different inferential regime from the classical one — not merely a quantitatively harder version. Procedures that succeed in low dimensions fail in high dimensions, and vice versa. The methods of this workshop are not refinements of low-dimensional procedures; they are the procedures appropriate to the regime in which most modern biological data now sit.

Lab 4 builds a sparse gene signature for the ALL-versus-AML distinction in the Golub dataset, using LASSO and elastic net with cross-validation and bootstrap stability analysis, and concludes by asking generative AI for a biological interpretation that the student then verifies against primary sources. The cycle — model, validate, interpret, verify — is the practical form of every claim made in this lecture.
