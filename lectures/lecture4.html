The Model That Memorizes Noise

Perfect Training, Catastrophic Prediction

Consider an ordinary-least-squares fit predicting patient outcomes from 500 gene-expression features on 80 patients.

. . .

  • Training \(R^2\): 0.99 — near-perfect fit on the training data
  • Test \(R^2\): \(-0.3\) — worse than predicting the sample mean

. . .

The model has memorized noise. When the number of features exceeds the number of samples, the OLS system is underdetermined: there are infinitely many coefficient vectors that interpolate the training data exactly. The fit reflects this degeneracy, not any property of the underlying biology.

. . .

This is the overfitting failure mode that regularization is designed to prevent.

The Regression Problem in High-D

We want to model a response \(y \in \mathbb{R}^n\) as a function of predictors \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}\):

\[y = X\beta + \varepsilon, \quad \varepsilon \sim N(0, \sigma^2 I)\]

. . .

When \(p > n\):

  • OLS \(\hat{\beta} = (X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty\) does not exist (\(X^TX\) is singular)
  • Even when \(p < n\) but \(p\) is close to \(n\), OLS overfits: high variance, poor prediction
  • Need to regularize: trade some bias for reduced variance

Ridge Regression

Add an \(\ell_2\) penalty:

\[\hat{\beta}_{\text{ridge}} = \arg\min_\beta \left\{ \frac{1}{2n}\|y - X\beta\|_2^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2} \|\beta\|_2^2 \right\}\]

. . .

Closed-form solution: \(\hat{\beta}_{\text{ridge}} = (X^TX + n\lambda I)^{-1}X^Ty\)

(All three methods—ridge, LASSO, elastic net—use the glmnet convention with \(1/(2n)\) scaling on the loss, so \(\lambda\) is comparable across methods.)

Properties

  • Shrinks all coefficients toward zero (but none exactly to zero)
  • The penalty term makes the system invertible even when \(p > n\)
  • Equivalent to a Bayesian posterior with \(\beta_j \sim N(0, \tau^2)\) prior, where \(\lambda = \sigma^2/\tau^2\)

Ridge: Bias-Variance Tradeoff

As \(\lambda\) increases:

  • Bias increases: coefficients shrink toward zero
  • Variance decreases: estimates become more stable

. . .

There exists an optimal \(\lambda\) that minimizes prediction error. The mean squared error:

\[\text{MSE}(\hat{\beta}_{\text{ridge}}) = \text{Bias}^2 + \text{Variance}\]

Ridge is optimal when many predictors have small effects (no true sparsity).

The Diamond vs. the Sphere

A Geometric Account of Sparsity

Why does replacing \(\|\beta\|_2^2\) with \(\|\beta\|_1\) — substituting a sphere for a diamond — drive coefficients to exactly zero?

. . .

The mechanism is the geometry of the constraint set: the \(\ell_1\) ball has corners on every coordinate axis; the \(\ell_2\) ball is smooth.

. . .

As the level sets of the squared-error loss expand outward from the OLS solution, they generally first contact the \(\ell_1\) ball at a vertex, at which one or more coordinates are exactly zero. The smooth \(\ell_2\) ball has no such distinguished points.

. . .

In \(p\) dimensions, the \(\ell_1\) ball has \(2p\) vertices — one per signed axis — and the probability of contact at a vertex grows with dimension.

The LASSO

Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (Tibshirani, 1996):

\[\hat{\beta}_{\text{lasso}} = \arg\min_\beta \left\{ \frac{1}{2n}\|y - X\beta\|_2^2 + \lambda \|\beta\|_1 \right\}\]

. . .

Key difference from ridge

The \(\ell_1\) penalty \(\|\beta\|_1 = \sum_j |\beta_j|\) produces sparse solutions: many \(\hat{\beta}_j = 0\) exactly.

LASSO simultaneously performs estimation and variable selection.

Geometry: Why \(\ell_1\) Gives Sparsity

The constraint region for \(\ell_1\) is a diamond (cross-polytope). The constraint for \(\ell_2\) is a sphere.

The OLS solution, projected onto the constraint set, is more likely to hit a corner of the diamond (where coordinates are zero) than the smooth surface of the sphere.

. . .

In \(p\) dimensions: the \(\ell_1\) ball has \(2p\) vertices (one per axis), making sparse intersections very likely.

LASSO: Solution Path

As \(\lambda\) decreases from \(\lambda_{\max}\) (where \(\hat{\beta} = 0\)) to 0:

  • Coefficients enter the model one at a time
  • The regularization path \(\hat{\beta}(\lambda)\) is piecewise linear (Efron et al., 2004, LARS)
  • Can compute the entire path efficiently

Choosing \(\lambda\): Cross-Validation

\(K\)-fold cross-validation:

  1. Split data into \(K\) folds (typically \(K = 10\))
  2. For each \(\lambda\): fit on \(K-1\) folds, predict on held-out fold
  3. Average the prediction error across folds
  4. Choose \(\lambda\) that minimizes CV error

. . .

Two common choices

  • \(\lambda_{\min}\): minimizes CV error (best prediction)
  • \(\lambda_{1\text{se}}\): largest \(\lambda\) within 1 SE of the minimum (more parsimonious model)

Elastic Net

A convex combination of the ridge and LASSO penalties (Zou & Hastie, 2005):

\[\hat{\beta}_{\text{enet}} = \arg\min_\beta \left\{ \frac{1}{2n}\|y - X\beta\|_2^2 + \lambda\left[\alpha\|\beta\|_1 + \frac{1-\alpha}{2}\|\beta\|_2^2\right] \right\}\]

  • \(\alpha = 1\): LASSO
  • \(\alpha = 0\): Ridge
  • \(0 < \alpha < 1\): Elastic net

. . .

When to use elastic net: Correlated predictors (common in genomics). LASSO tends to arbitrarily select one from a group of correlated variables; elastic net selects the group.

Penalized Regression: Summary

Method Penalty Sparsity? Best when…
Ridge \(\frac{1}{2}\|\beta\|_2^2\) No Many small effects
LASSO \(\|\beta\|_1\) Yes Few large effects
Elastic Net \(\alpha\|\beta\|_1 + \frac{1-\alpha}{2}\|\beta\|_2^2\) Yes Correlated predictors

. . .

All are available in glmnet (R) and scikit-learn (Python).

Theoretical Guarantees

LASSO consistency (Zhao & Yu, 2006)

Under the irrepresentable condition and sufficient signal strength, LASSO recovers the true support (nonzero coefficients) with probability \(\to 1\) as \(n \to \infty\).

Prediction error bounds

Under restricted eigenvalue conditions, LASSO achieves prediction error:

\[\frac{1}{n}\|X\hat{\beta}_{\text{lasso}} - X\beta^*\|_2^2 = O\left(\frac{s \log p}{n}\right)\]

where \(s\) = number of true nonzero coefficients. This is near-optimal—you pay only \(\log p\), not \(p\).

Two Case Studies: Validation Determines Outcome

MammaPrint: Regularization in Clinical Practice

In 2002, van ’t Veer and colleagues measured 25,000 genes on 78 breast-cancer patients and identified a 70-gene signature predicting metastasis through a supervised feature-selection procedure.

. . .

The signature was commercialized as MammaPrint, cleared by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration, and prospectively validated in the MINDACT trial (Cardoso et al., 2016, NEJM): 6,693 patients with high clinical but low genomic risk were randomized to chemotherapy or no chemotherapy.

. . .

Result: a 94.7 percent five-year distant-metastasis-free survival rate without chemotherapy. Approximately 46 percent of women who would otherwise have received chemotherapy were spared it — on the basis of a model built on 25,000 variables and 78 patients.

The Duke Episode: When the Same Method Fails

At Duke University, Anil Potti and colleagues used gene-expression signatures to predict tumor response to specific chemotherapy agents. Clinical trials enrolled patients on the basis of those signatures.

. . .

Statisticians at MD Anderson — Keith Baggerly and Kevin Coombes — spent several years attempting to reproduce the Duke analyses (Baggerly & Coombes, 2009, Ann. Appl. Stat.). They identified mislabeled samples, off-by-one errors in gene indices, and conclusions that were the inverse of what the data actually supported.

. . .

The episode resulted in eighteen retracted papers and the revocation of Potti’s medical license. Patients had been assigned to treatments on the basis of erroneous models.

The Same Method, Different Outcomes

The Duke and MammaPrint analyses applied the same class of methods to the same kind of data for the same kind of question. The differences lay entirely in execution:

. . .

  • Validation: MammaPrint was tested in a 6,693-patient prospective randomized trial. The Duke signatures were not subjected to comparable external validation.
  • Reproducibility: The Duke analysis could not be reproduced because code and data were not shared. The 70-gene signature was independently replicated.
  • Information leakage: The Duke pipeline leaked information between training and test sets. The MammaPrint pipeline did not.

. . .

The lesson is that regularization is a tool, not a guarantee. A model is only as reliable as the analysis pipeline that produced it.

A Reproducibility Caveat: LASSO Selection Instability

Even under correct application, LASSO exhibits a notable instability:

. . .

When predictors are correlated — as gene-expression measurements typically are — LASSO selects one representative from each correlated group, with the choice determined essentially arbitrarily by sampling noise. Resampling produces different selected subsets while leaving prediction accuracy nearly unchanged.

. . .

The implication is that bootstrap stability analysis is necessary to determine which variables are consistently selected, as opposed to selected in any one fit. Elastic net mitigates this via its grouping property.

Generative AI in High-Dimensional Analysis

Large language models (Claude, ChatGPT, and others) can write and debug analysis code, summarize statistical output, suggest analysis strategies, and produce first-pass biological interpretations.

. . .

They are well suited to code generation, syntax lookup, and boilerplate. They are unreliable for statistical judgment, numerical computation, and novel biological claims: generated code may contain subtle bugs and reported gene functions may be fabricated.

Common Errors to Watch For

Error Why it matters How you catch it
No multiple testing correction Hundreds of false positives Check for p.adjust() or equivalent
Uses theoretical null when empirical null is needed Inflated false discovery rate Look at the \(z\)-score histogram
Runs t-SNE on raw 20K dimensions Slow, noisy, misleading Check if PCA was applied first
Doesn’t set scale. = TRUE in PCA Variables on different scales dominate Read the code carefully
Uses lambda.min without justification Overly complex model Is the sparser lambda.1se model adequate?
Hallucinated gene functions Wrong biological conclusions Verify against GeneCards, NCBI Gene, PubMed

Verifying Biological Interpretations

After identifying a gene set, you might ask an AI to describe their functions. Always cross-reference the response:

  1. Pick at least 3 genes
  2. Look them up on GeneCards or NCBI Gene
  3. Check: Did the AI get the function right? Did it invent a citation?

Other Modern Approaches (Brief Survey)

Deep learning for high-D biology

  • Autoencoders: Nonlinear dimension reduction; used in single-cell analysis (e.g., scVI)
  • Variational autoencoders (VAEs): Probabilistic; learn latent representations
  • Transformers: Protein structure prediction (AlphaFold), gene expression modeling (Geneformer)

. . .

When do these beat classical methods?

  • Very large \(n\) (thousands of samples/cells)
  • Complex nonlinear relationships
  • Transfer learning from pre-trained models

For \(n \leq\) a few hundred (typical in many BTP settings), penalized regression and empirical Bayes methods often perform comparably or better, and are much more interpretable.

Bringing It All Together

The complete high-dimensional analysis toolkit:

Day Topic Key Idea
1 Multiple testing & FDR Control false discoveries when testing thousands of hypotheses
1 Empirical Bayes Use the aggregate data to estimate prior, then individualize
2 PCA & UMAP Find low-dimensional structure; visualize
2 Penalized regression Predict and select variables when \(p \gg n\)
2 LLMs as tools Accelerate coding and interpretation; verify everything

Discussion

Take a few minutes on each of these before we move to the lab.

Why not OLS?

You have \(n = 80\) samples and \(p = 5{,}000\) gene expression measurements. Why can’t you just run ordinary least squares?

. . .

  • Mathematical: \(X^TX\) is at most rank 80, so it’s singular—\((X^TX)^{-1}\) doesn’t exist.
  • Practical: Even if \(p\) were just slightly less than \(n\), OLS would overfit catastrophically—memorizing noise rather than learning signal.

Ridge at the extremes

Ridge regression minimizes \(\frac{1}{2n}\|y - X\beta\|_2^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2}\|\beta\|_2^2\).

Discuss: What happens to \(\hat{\beta}_{\text{ridge}}\) as \(\lambda \to 0\)? As \(\lambda \to \infty\)?

. . .

  • \(\lambda \to 0\): Ridge approaches OLS (minimum-norm solution when \(p > n\)). Maximum variance, minimum bias.
  • \(\lambda \to \infty\): All coefficients shrink to zero. Zero variance, maximum bias. The model predicts the mean \(\bar{y}\) for every observation.

Why does LASSO give zeros?

Discuss: Explain geometrically why the \(\ell_1\) penalty gives exactly sparse solutions but the \(\ell_2\) penalty does not.

. . .

The \(\ell_1\) constraint set is a diamond (corners on the axes). The \(\ell_2\) constraint set is a sphere (smooth, no corners). The OLS contours typically hit the diamond at a corner, where one or more coordinates are exactly zero. The sphere has no corners to hit.

In \(p\) dimensions: the \(\ell_1\) ball has \(2p\) vertices, all on coordinate axes. High-dimensional geometry makes corner intersections even more likely.

Which \(\lambda\)?

Cross-validation gives \(\lambda_{\min}\) (45 genes) and \(\lambda_{1\text{se}}\) (12 genes). You’re writing a paper to identify a gene signature.

Discuss: Which do you report?

. . .

\(\lambda_{1\text{se}}\). Its prediction error is statistically indistinguishable from \(\lambda_{\min}\), but the model is much sparser. A 12-gene signature is more interpretable, more reproducible, and more practical for a diagnostic panel than a 45-gene signature.

Summary

  1. Ridge, LASSO, and elastic net address the \(p > n\) regression problem through regularization.
  2. Use LASSO for sparse solutions, ridge when many small effects are anticipated, and elastic net when predictors are correlated.
  3. Cross-validation selects the regularization strength.
  4. LLMs are useful for code generation and interpretation but require critical evaluation of every claim.
  5. Classical methods remain competitive for moderate \(n\); deep learning advantages emerge with very large datasets.

## LASSO: Solution Path

- The **regularization path** $\hat{\beta}(\lambda)$ is piecewise linear ([Efron et al., 2004, LARS](https://doi.org/10.1214/009053604000000067))
- Can compute the entire path efficiently

```{python}
#| echo: false
#| fig-height: 4
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.linear_model import lasso_path

rng = np.random.default_rng(1)
n, p = 100, 200
X_sim = rng.normal(size=(n, p))
beta_true = np.concatenate([np.full(5, 3.0), np.full(5, -2.0), np.zeros(p - 10)])
y_sim = X_sim @ beta_true + rng.normal(size=n)

alphas, coefs, _ = lasso_path(X_sim, y_sim, n_alphas=100)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4))
for i in range(min(p, 20)):  # plot first 20 features for clarity
    ax.semilogx(alphas, coefs[i], linewidth=0.8)
ax.set_xlabel("λ (regularization strength)")
ax.set_ylabel("Coefficient value")
ax.set_title("LASSO Path")
ax.invert_xaxis()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

## Discussion

*Take a few minutes on each of these before we move to the lab.*

### Why not OLS?

You have $n = 80$ samples and $p = 5{,}000$ gene expression measurements. Why can't you just run ordinary least squares?

. . .

- **Mathematical**: $X^TX$ is at most rank 80, so it's singular—$(X^TX)^{-1}$ doesn't exist.
- **Practical**: Even if $p$ were just slightly less than $n$, OLS would overfit catastrophically—memorizing noise rather than learning signal.

---

### Ridge at the extremes

Ridge regression minimizes $\frac{1}{2n}\|y - X\beta\|_2^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2}\|\beta\|_2^2$.

**Discuss**: What happens to $\hat{\beta}_{\text{ridge}}$ as $\lambda \to 0$? As $\lambda \to \infty$?

. . .

- $\lambda \to 0$: Ridge approaches OLS (minimum-norm solution when $p > n$). Maximum variance, minimum bias.
- $\lambda \to \infty$: All coefficients shrink to zero. Zero variance, maximum bias. The model predicts the mean $\bar{y}$ for every observation.

---

### Why does LASSO give zeros?

**Discuss**: Explain geometrically why the $\ell_1$ penalty gives exactly sparse solutions but the $\ell_2$ penalty does not.

. . .

The $\ell_1$ constraint set is a diamond (corners on the axes). The $\ell_2$ constraint set is a sphere (smooth, no corners). The OLS contours typically hit the diamond at a corner, where one or more coordinates are exactly zero. The sphere has no corners to hit.

In $p$ dimensions: the $\ell_1$ ball has $2p$ vertices, all on coordinate axes. High-dimensional geometry makes corner intersections even more likely.

---

### Which $\lambda$?

Cross-validation gives $\lambda_{\min}$ (45 genes) and $\lambda_{1\text{se}}$ (12 genes). You're writing a paper to identify a gene signature.

**Discuss**: Which do you report?

. . .

$\lambda_{1\text{se}}$. Its prediction error is statistically indistinguishable from $\lambda_{\min}$, but the model is much sparser. A 12-gene signature is more interpretable, more reproducible, and more practical for a diagnostic panel than a 45-gene signature.

## Summary

1. **Ridge, LASSO, and elastic net** address the $p > n$ regression problem through regularization.
2. Use **LASSO** for sparse solutions, **ridge** when many small effects are anticipated, and **elastic net** when predictors are correlated.
3. **Cross-validation** selects the regularization strength.
4. **LLMs** are useful for code generation and interpretation but require critical evaluation of every claim.
5. Classical methods remain competitive for moderate $n$; deep learning advantages emerge with very large datasets.
