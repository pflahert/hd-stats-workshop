Lecture 3: The Map That Drew Itself

Dimension Reduction and the Geometry of High-Dimensional Data

Published

Invalid Date

From eigenfaces to genes

In 1991, Turk and Pentland applied principal component analysis to a database of face photographs. The leading principal components — diffuse, low-resolution images they termed eigenfaces — sufficed to reconstruct any face in the database from approximately 100 numerical coefficients in place of the thousands of pixels in the original image. The reduction was dramatic and the result was new: dimension reduction had moved from a mathematical construct to a practical tool for representing high-dimensional objects.

The idea is now ubiquitous. In single-cell genomics, where 30,000 transcript abundances per cell are typical, the standard pipeline begins by compressing the expression matrix to 30 to 50 principal components before any clustering, embedding, or downstream analysis. What worked for faces works, with appropriate modifications, for cells, tissues, brain images, and protein structures. The unifying assumption is that high-dimensional data often live near a low-dimensional manifold; the methods of this lecture identify that manifold and project the data onto it.

The lecture covers three topics. The first is principal component analysis, including its formal definition, the relationship to the singular value decomposition, and the random-matrix complications that arise in the \(p \gg n\) regime. The second is the two principal nonlinear methods for dimension reduction — t-SNE and UMAP — and the systematic ways in which they mislead. The third is a practical workflow that combines the linear and nonlinear methods to obtain useful representations without obvious artifacts.

Principal component analysis

The goal is to represent data \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}\) in a lower-dimensional space \(\mathbb{R}^k\) with \(k \ll p\) while preserving as much of the data’s structure as possible. PCA realizes this goal under a precise notion of “structure”: the variance of linear combinations of the variables.

For centered data \(X\), the first principal component \(\mathbf{v}_1\) is the unit-norm direction that maximizes the variance of the projection \(X\mathbf{v}\):

\[\mathbf{v}_1 = \arg\max_{\|\mathbf{v}\|=1} \mathbf{v}^\top \hat\Sigma \mathbf{v}, \qquad \hat\Sigma = \frac{1}{n-1} X^\top X.\]

The solution is the eigenvector of \(\hat\Sigma\) corresponding to the largest eigenvalue. Subsequent principal components are defined recursively as the directions orthogonal to those already chosen that maximize the residual variance.

Computationally, PCA is most cleanly expressed through the singular value decomposition of the data matrix:

\[X = U D V^\top,\]

where \(U\) has orthonormal columns of length \(n\) (the left singular vectors), \(D\) is the diagonal matrix of singular values \(d_1 \geq d_2 \geq \cdots \geq 0\), and \(V\) has orthonormal columns of length \(p\) (the right singular vectors). The eigenvalues of the sample covariance matrix are \(\lambda_j = d_j^2/(n-1)\); the principal-component scores are \(Z = XV = UD\), and the loadings \(V\) identify which variables contribute to each component.

The optimality of PCA is precisely characterized by the Eckart–Young theorem (1936): the rank-\(k\) matrix that minimizes the Frobenius reconstruction error of \(X\) is obtained by truncating the SVD to its top \(k\) singular values. PCA is therefore the optimal linear dimension reduction — a guarantee that does not extend to nonlinear methods.

Choosing the number of components

The number of components \(k\) to retain is a perennial question. Three approaches are common.

The scree plot displays the eigenvalues \(\lambda_j\) (or the proportion of variance they explain) against the index \(j\). An “elbow” — a point at which subsequent eigenvalues fall off sharply — suggests a natural cutoff. The diagnostic is informal but often useful.

The proportion of variance explained,

\[\mathrm{PVE}(k) = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^k \lambda_j}{\sum_{j=1}^p \lambda_j},\]

provides a quantitative summary; thresholds in the range of 70 to 90 percent are common.

The Marchenko–Pastur threshold offers a more principled criterion in the \(p \gg n\) regime. Under the null hypothesis that all variables are independent noise, the bulk of the eigenvalues of the sample covariance matrix is distributed within \([\max(0,(1-\sqrt\gamma)^2), (1+\sqrt\gamma)^2]\) as \(p, n \to \infty\) with \(p/n \to \gamma\). Eigenvalues exceeding the upper edge \((1+\sqrt\gamma)^2\) correspond to components that lie above the noise floor and are therefore candidates for true signal.

Parallel analysis, which compares the observed eigenvalues to those obtained by permuting each column of \(X\) independently, provides a nonparametric counterpart. Both methods address the same question: which eigenvalues exceed what would be expected from noise alone.

The detection phase transition

A more profound limitation of PCA in the \(p \gg n\) regime was established by Baik, Ben Arous, and Péché (2005). For a signal-plus-noise model in which the population covariance matrix has a single spike of magnitude \(\ell\) above the identity baseline, the leading eigenvalue of the sample covariance matrix is detectable, in the asymptotic limit \(p/n \to \gamma\), only if

\[\ell > 1 + \sqrt\gamma.\]

Below this threshold, the signal eigenvalue is absorbed into the bulk noise spectrum and becomes indistinguishable from a noise eigenvalue. The leading sample principal component, in such cases, has zero asymptotic correlation with the true signal direction.

The numerical implications are sobering. With \(p = 10{,}000\) and \(n = 100\), \(\gamma = 100\) and the threshold is \(1 + \sqrt{100} = 11\). A signal of magnitude smaller than 11 (relative to the noise variance of 1) is, in the asymptotic limit, undetectable by PCA — no matter how cleverly the analysis is performed. The threshold is a property of the geometry of the problem, not of any particular procedure.

The result has practical consequences. In single-cell genomics, where tens of thousands of genes are measured on hundreds or thousands of cells, the leading principal components reflect the strongest biological structure (cell type, developmental stage), but weaker structure may lie below the detection limit and require additional information — for example, prior knowledge of marker genes — to recover.

Biplots: reading PCA visually

The biplot is a simultaneous visualization of sample scores and variable loadings on a single set of axes. The scores show the projected positions of the samples in the first two principal components; arrows superimposed on the same axes indicate the loading of each variable on those components. Arrows pointing in the same direction as a cluster of points identify the variables that drive the cluster’s separation along the relevant axes.

The interpretation is geometric and therefore concrete. If a cluster of samples lies in the upper right of the biplot, and the arrows for genes A and B point in that direction, then A and B are highly expressed in that cluster relative to the rest. The biplot offers something neither PCA scores nor loadings alone provide: a single picture in which the data and the variables that organize them appear together.

When t-SNE misleads

PCA is constrained to linear dimension reduction. When the data lie on a curved manifold — common in single-cell biology, where developmental trajectories and continuous lineage transitions are nonlinear — PCA may fail to expose the structure. Nonlinear methods address this limitation. The two most widely used are t-distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) and Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP).

The methods are powerful but have systematic failure modes that are widely underappreciated. Wattenberg, Viégas, and Johnson (2016) documented these failure modes in an interactive article that has become required reading. Among the points they established:

  • t-SNE can produce apparent clusters from data that are uniformly distributed in the original space.
  • It can partition a single connected population into two visually distinct clusters.
  • It can place distant groups in adjacent positions.
  • Cluster sizes and between-cluster distances in the embedding are not interpretable as quantities in the original data.

These behaviors are not bugs. They are consequences of t-SNE’s optimization criterion, which preserves local neighbor relationships at the expense of global geometry. The implication is that a t-SNE plot is a sketch of the data, not a photograph; it is a tool for hypothesis generation, not for hypothesis confirmation.

t-SNE, UMAP, and diffusion maps

t-SNE (van der Maaten and Hinton, 2008) constructs pairwise affinities \(p_{ij}\) in the high-dimensional space using Gaussian kernels and matches them to affinities \(q_{ij}\) in the low-dimensional space using a Student-\(t\) distribution. The objective is the Kullback–Leibler divergence

\[\mathrm{KL}(P \| Q) = \sum_{i \neq j} p_{ij} \log \frac{p_{ij}}{q_{ij}},\]

minimized by gradient descent. The heavy tails of the Student-\(t\) distribution in low dimensions allow points that are far apart in the original space to be pushed even further apart in the embedding, which improves cluster separation. Two parameters dominate behavior: the perplexity, which controls the effective number of neighbors used to construct \(p_{ij}\), and the number of optimization iterations.

UMAP (McInnes, Healy, and Melville, 2018) constructs a fuzzy simplicial complex in the high-dimensional space — a weighted graph encoding local neighbor relationships — and optimizes a low-dimensional layout to match. Compared to t-SNE, UMAP tends to preserve global structure somewhat better, scales more favorably to large \(n\), and admits a theoretical interpretation in terms of Riemannian geometry and algebraic topology. Two principal parameters: n_neighbors, analogous to perplexity, and min_dist, which controls how tightly points cluster in the embedding.

Diffusion maps (Coifman and Lafon, 2006) constitute a third nonlinear method, based on the spectral decomposition of a Markov transition matrix on the data graph. The leading eigenvectors of this matrix provide low-dimensional coordinates that respect the diffusion geometry of the manifold; the method is particularly natural for data with continuous trajectory structure.

The three methods differ in their commitments. PCA preserves variance and is linear and deterministic. t-SNE preserves local neighbor identities at the expense of global geometry. UMAP balances local and global structure with greater fidelity than t-SNE in most settings. Diffusion maps preserve a particular notion of intrinsic geometry. None is uniformly best; the choice depends on the structure to be exposed.

A practical workflow

The combination of linear and nonlinear methods has become standard in high-dimensional biological analysis. The recommended workflow is:

  1. Apply PCA first. Use the leading components to inspect for batch effects, identify the variance structure, and choose a number \(k\) of components to retain — typically 20 to 50 in single-cell applications.
  2. Apply a nonlinear method (t-SNE or UMAP) to the top \(k\) principal components rather than to the raw \(p\)-dimensional data.
  3. Interpret the resulting embedding as a tool for visualization and hypothesis generation, not as a quantitatively faithful representation of the original geometry.

The motivation for the PCA-first step is twofold. PCA removes the high-dimensional noise that distorts t-SNE and UMAP, sharpening the structure that survives. It also dramatically reduces the computational cost: fitting t-SNE or UMAP on 50 dimensions is much faster than on 20,000. The Johnson–Lindenstrauss lemma (Johnson and Lindenstrauss, 1984) provides a related theoretical assurance — random projections approximately preserve pairwise distances — and PCA is, in the relevant noise regime, strictly better than random projection.

Cautions

A small set of conclusions are systematically invalid from t-SNE or UMAP plots alone:

  • “These two clusters are far apart” — distances between clusters are not reliably preserved.
  • “This cluster is larger or denser than that one” — cluster sizes in the embedding are not preserved.
  • “There are exactly \(k\) clusters” — apparent clusters can be artifacts of perplexity, initialization, or convergence.
  • “The trajectory runs from A to B” — without quantitative validation, trajectory inferences from embeddings are speculative.

The general rule: nonlinear embeddings are tools for exploration. Quantitative claims about the geometry of the data — distances, sizes, counts of clusters, trajectories — require validation by other methods.

A related caution applies to PCA itself. In the \(p \gg n\) regime, the Marchenko–Pastur law inflates eigenvalues even under pure noise: with \(\gamma = 200\), the leading noise eigenvalue can exceed \((1+\sqrt{200})^2 \approx 229\) on the covariance scale. A leading PC that explains 15 percent of the total variance is impressive against the naive baseline of \(1/\min(n,p) \approx 1\) percent expected under independence, but the proper baseline in the \(p \gg n\) regime is the Marchenko–Pastur upper edge, not the naive one. Scree plots and parallel analysis remain the most reliable diagnostics.

Connections to factor models

PCA is closely related to the linear factor model

\[X = FL^\top + E,\]

in which \(F\) (\(n \times k\)) contains factor scores, \(L\) (\(p \times k\)) contains factor loadings, and \(E\) contains noise. When the noise is homoscedastic (\(E_{ij} \sim N(0,\sigma^2)\)), PCA recovers the factors. When the noise is heteroscedastic, probabilistic PCA and factor analysis are more appropriate. In genomics, surrogate variable analysis (SVA) extends this framework to estimate latent confounders that distort gene-level inferences if left uncontrolled.

The factor-model perspective is useful because it makes explicit a question that PCA tends to obscure: are the leading components capturing biology, or are they capturing nuisance variation (batch, technical artifact, latent population structure)? The same eigenvector decomposition can produce either, and only domain knowledge — which the math itself does not supply — distinguishes them.

Summary and outlook

Six points constitute the lecture’s content. First, PCA is the default first step in dimension reduction: fast, interpretable, and optimal for linear structure under the Eckart–Young theorem. Second, scree plots and the Marchenko–Pastur threshold support principled selection of the number of components. Third, t-SNE and UMAP reveal nonlinear structure but distort distances, cluster sizes, and counts of apparent clusters; their plots are sketches, not photographs. Fourth, the recommended workflow is PCA first, then a nonlinear method on the top principal components. Fifth, nonlinear embeddings should be treated as instruments for exploration rather than confirmation. Sixth, in the \(p \gg n\) regime, random matrix theory imposes fundamental limits on what PCA can detect; weak signals are not merely hard to find — they are absorbed into the noise spectrum.
