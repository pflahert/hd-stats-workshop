Lecture 2: False Discovery Rates & Empirical Bayes

From Benjamini–Hochberg to Efron’s Local FDR

Published

Invalid Date

The Histogram That Tells You Everything

A Picture Worth 10,000 Tests

A \(p\)-value histogram from a gene-expression study comparing two cancer subtypes has two components:

  • The flat part (uniform across \([0,1]\)): null genes — no real signal
  • The spike near 0: true discoveries — genes that actually differ

. . .

The proportion of true nulls \(\pi_0\) is visible — it is the height of the flat region.

. . .

That the histogram itself encodes the answer is the foundation of this lecture. Translating the visual intuition into a rigorous procedure took several decades.

Recap from Lecture 1

  • High-dimensional data: \(p \gg n\)
  • Multiple testing: \(m\) simultaneous tests generate many false positives
  • FWER (Bonferroni) is too conservative for discovery settings
  • FDR = \(E[V / (R \vee 1)]\) is the right error metric

. . .

This lecture: How to control FDR, and the deeper theory behind it.

The Benjamini–Hochberg (BH) Procedure

Given \(m\) hypothesis tests with \(p\)-values \(p_1, \ldots, p_m\):

  1. Order the \(p\)-values: \(p_{(1)} \leq p_{(2)} \leq \cdots \leq p_{(m)}\)
  2. Find the largest \(k\) such that \(p_{(k)} \leq \frac{k}{m} \alpha\)
  3. Reject all \(H_{0(i)}\) for \(i = 1, \ldots, k\)

. . .

Theorem (Benjamini & Hochberg, 1995): Under independence, \(\text{FDR} \leq \frac{m_0}{m} \alpha \leq \alpha\).

BH: Geometric Interpretation

Plot \(p_{(i)}\) vs. \(i/m\). The BH procedure finds the rightmost point where the \(p\)-value curve dips below the line \(y = \alpha \cdot x\).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(42)
m, m1 = 1000, 50
p_null = rng.uniform(size=m - m1)
p_alt = rng.beta(0.1, 1, size=m1)  # small p-values for alternatives
pvals_sim = np.sort(np.concatenate([p_null, p_alt]))

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4))
ax.scatter(np.arange(1, m + 1) / m, pvals_sim, s=3, color="black")
ax.plot([0, 1], [0, 0.10], color="red", linewidth=2, label="BH line (slope = α)")
ax.set_xlabel("i / m")
ax.set_ylabel("Ordered p-value")
ax.set_title("BH procedure (α = 0.10)")
ax.legend(loc="upper left")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

BH Under Dependence

The original BH result assumes independence of test statistics. In genomics, genes are often correlated.

. . .

Benjamini & Yekutieli (2001): Under arbitrary dependence, replace \(\alpha\) with \(\alpha / \sum_{i=1}^m 1/i \approx \alpha / \ln(m)\).

. . .

But: Under positive regression dependency on a subset (PRDS)—a condition often satisfied in practice—the original BH procedure still controls FDR at level \(\alpha\).

The Prior You Didn’t Know You Had

Stein’s Paradox: Borrowing Strength

In 1956, Charles Stein established a result that reshaped the statistical landscape:

The sample mean is inadmissible for estimating 3 or more means simultaneously.

. . .

Efron & Morris (1977) illustrated the principle with baseball. End-of-season batting averages are better predicted by shrinking each player’s early-season average toward the grand mean across players — despite the players being unrelated.

. . .

The James–Stein estimator (James & Stein, 1961): \(\hat{\theta}_i^{JS} = \bar{y} + \left(1 - \frac{(d-3)\sigma^2}{\sum_j (y_j - \bar{y})^2}\right)(y_i - \bar{y})\),

where \(d \geq 4\) is the number of means estimated.

. . .

This is the founding insight of empirical Bayes: the other observations carry information about the observation under analysis. Efron generalized the idea — from baseball averages to 10,000 genes.

From Baseball to Genes

In classical statistics each gene is tested in isolation; the test for gene #4,237 uses no information from genes #1–4,236.

. . .

Efron observed that, when 10,000 genes are tested simultaneously, the other 9,999 results carry information about any particular gene — just as other batters’ averages refine the estimate for any single batter.

. . .

If most genes show no signal, the prior probability that any particular gene is non-null is low. The data themselves reveal the prior; the analyst need not specify one.

. . .

“Letting the data determine the prior turns Bayesian inference from a subjective into a largely objective technique.” — Efron, Large-Scale Inference, Ch. 1

Storey’s Approach: Estimating \(\pi_0\)

BH controls FDR at level \(\frac{m_0}{m}\alpha\), which is conservative when \(m_0 < m\).

Storey (2002): Estimate \(\pi_0 = m_0/m\) and use an adaptive threshold.

For a tuning parameter \(\lambda\) (e.g., \(\lambda = 0.5\)):

\[\hat{\pi}_0(\lambda) = \frac{\#\{p_i > \lambda\}}{m(1 - \lambda)}\]

. . .

Intuition: \(p\)-values above \(\lambda\) are mostly from true nulls (which are uniform). Count them.

The R package qvalue implements this with automatic \(\lambda\) selection.

The \(q\)-value

Definition (Storey, 2003): The \(q\)-value of test \(i\) is the minimum FDR at which test \(i\) would be called significant.

\[q_i = \min_{t \geq p_i} \widehat{\text{FDR}}(t)\]

. . .

Analogous to a \(p\)-value, but for FDR instead of Type I error:

  • \(p\)-value: “If I reject this one test, what’s the false positive probability?”
  • \(q\)-value: “If I reject all tests at least this significant, what fraction are false positives?”

Efron’s Two-Groups Model

A more foundational perspective (Efron, 2004, JASA; developed fully in Efron, 2008, Statistical Science; and Efron, 2010, Large-Scale Inference, Ch. 2–4).

Each of the \(m\) cases belongs to one of two groups:

  • Null group (proportion \(\pi_0\)): \(z_i \sim f_0(z)\), typically \(N(0,1)\)
  • Non-null group (proportion \(1 - \pi_0\)): \(z_i \sim f_1(z)\)

The mixture density of the observed \(z\)-scores is:

\[f(z) = \pi_0 f_0(z) + (1 - \pi_0) f_1(z)\]

Estimating the Mixture

Key idea

We observe \(m\) \(z\)-scores. With \(m\) large (thousands), we can estimate \(f(z)\) nonparametrically.

. . .

Efron’s approach:

  1. Estimate \(f(z)\) from a histogram or density estimate of the observed \(z\)-scores
  2. Assume \(f_0\) is known (or estimate it—the “empirical null”)
  3. Estimate \(\pi_0\) from the central peak of the histogram

. . .

This is empirical Bayes: we use the aggregate data to estimate the prior, then apply Bayes’ rule case by case.

The Local False Discovery Rate

\[\text{fdr}(z) = P(\text{null} \mid Z = z) = \frac{\pi_0 f_0(z)}{f(z)}\]

. . .

This is a posterior probability—the chance that a specific case with \(z\)-score \(z\) is a false discovery.

Contrast with the (tail-area) FDR, which is an expected proportion over a set of rejections.

. . .

Relationship

\[\text{FDR}(z_0) = E[\text{fdr}(Z) \mid Z \geq z_0] = \frac{\int_{z_0}^\infty \text{fdr}(z) f(z) \, dz}{\int_{z_0}^\infty f(z) \, dz}\]

The tail-area FDR is the average of the local FDR over the rejection region.

Connections to Bayesian Thinking

The two-groups model is inherently Bayesian:

  • Prior: \(P(\text{null}) = \pi_0\)
  • Likelihood: \(f_0(z)\) under the null, \(f_1(z)\) under the alternative
  • Posterior: \(\text{fdr}(z) = P(\text{null} \mid z)\)

. . .

But it’s empirical Bayes: instead of specifying a prior, we estimate \(\pi_0\) and \(f_1\) from the data.

When \(N(0,1)\) Lies to You

A Cautionary Case: Efron’s Prostate Cancer Reanalysis

Efron analyzed a prostate-cancer microarray study (Singh et al., 2002): 6,033 genes, 102 patients.

The test statistics were converted to \(z\)-scores under the expectation that they would follow \(N(0,1)\) under the null. The central peak of the empirical histogram was instead wider than expected: the empirical null was closer to \(N(\hat{\delta}_0, \hat{\sigma}_0^2)\) with \(\hat{\sigma}_0 > 1\).

. . .

Applying the theoretical \(N(0,1)\) null would overstate significance: genes whose \(z\)-scores fell in the (incorrectly narrow) tails would be declared discoveries despite being consistent with the actual, wider null.

. . .

The empirical null corrected the analysis and demonstrated that the theoretical \(N(0,1)\) null cannot be assumed without verification. The estimated parameters depend on the dataset and procedure; the central observation is that \(\hat{\sigma}_0 \neq 1\) in many real applications.

The Empirical Null

Why the theoretical null might be wrong

Efron observed that in many real datasets, the central peak of the \(z\)-score histogram is wider than \(N(0,1)\).

Possible causes:

  • Unobserved covariates (batch effects, population structure)
  • Correlation among tests
  • Model misspecification

. . .

Empirical null

Fit \(f_0(z) = N(\hat{\delta}_0, \hat{\sigma}_0^2)\) to the central portion of the observed \(z\)-score distribution.

When \(\hat{\sigma}_0 > 1\), the theoretical null is too narrow and the analysis would yield an excess of false discoveries.

Empirical Null: Illustration

from scipy.stats import norm

rng = np.random.default_rng(123)
m, m1 = 5000, 200
z_null = rng.normal(loc=0.2, scale=1.3, size=m - m1)  # empirical null ≠ N(0,1)
z_alt = rng.normal(loc=3, scale=1, size=m1)
z = np.concatenate([z_null, z_alt])

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4.5))
ax.hist(z, bins=80, density=True, color="lightblue", edgecolor="white")
x_grid = np.linspace(z.min(), z.max(), 300)
ax.plot(x_grid, norm.pdf(x_grid, 0, 1), color="red", linewidth=2, linestyle="--",
        label="Theoretical N(0,1)")
ax.plot(x_grid, norm.pdf(x_grid, 0.2, 1.3), color="blue", linewidth=2,
        label="Empirical null N(0.2, 1.3²)")
ax.set_title("Observed z-scores with empirical null")
ax.set_xlabel("z-score")
ax.legend(loc="upper right")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Efron’s Estimation Procedure

Step by step (Efron, Large-Scale Inference, Ch. 4–5)

  1. Convert test statistics to \(z\)-scores: \(z_i = \Phi^{-1}(1 - p_i)\) or use the statistic directly
  2. Estimate \(f(z)\) using Poisson regression on binned counts (Lindsey’s method)
  3. Estimate \(\pi_0\) and \(f_0\) (theoretical or empirical null)
  4. Compute \(\widehat{\text{fdr}}(z) = \hat{\pi}_0 \hat{f}_0(z) / \hat{f}(z)\)

. . .

The R package locfdr automates this.

When Does This Work?

Requirements for empirical Bayes FDR

  1. Large \(m\): Need thousands of tests to estimate \(f(z)\) well
  2. Exchangeability: Tests should be “comparable” (same type of measurement, similar noise structure)
  3. Mixture model is reasonable: The two-groups framework is a good approximation

. . .

When it can fail

  • Small \(m\) (say \(< 100\)): not enough data to estimate the mixture
  • Strong correlation structure that distorts the null distribution
  • Heterogeneous tests (mixing gene expression with methylation, for example)

Multiple Testing in Practice: A Decision Framework

Software Ecosystem

Method R Python
Bonferroni / Holm p.adjust() statsmodels.stats.multitest
Benjamini–Hochberg p.adjust(method="BH") multipletests(method='fdr_bh')
Storey \(q\)-values qvalue::qvalue() -- (custom or qvalue port)
Local FDR locfdr::locfdr() -- (custom implementation)
Empirical Bayes (limma) limma::eBayes() -- (use R via rpy2)

Summary

  1. BH procedure: Simple; controls FDR under independence or PRDS. The default tool for high-dimensional inference.
  2. Storey’s \(q\)-value: Adaptive FDR control using \(\hat{\pi}_0\). More powerful when most tests are null.
  3. Efron’s two-groups model: The foundational framework. The mixture \(f = \pi_0 f_0 + (1-\pi_0)f_1\) unifies the procedures above.
  4. Local FDR: A test-specific posterior probability of being null — the most informative single summary at the level of individual tests.
  5. Empirical null: Always assess whether \(f_0 = N(0,1)\) is appropriate; estimate it from the data when it is not.

```{python}
ax.plot(x_grid, norm.pdf(x_grid, 0.2, 1.3), color="blue", linewidth=2,
        label="Empirical null N(0.2, 1.3²)")
ax.set_title("Observed z-scores with empirical null")
ax.set_xlabel("z-score")
ax.legend(loc="upper right")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

## Efron's Estimation Procedure

### Step by step (Efron, *Large-Scale Inference*, Ch. 4–5)

1. Convert test statistics to $z$-scores: $z_i = \Phi^{-1}(1 - p_i)$ or use the statistic directly
2. Estimate $f(z)$ using Poisson regression on binned counts (Lindsey's method)
3. Estimate $\pi_0$ and $f_0$ (theoretical or empirical null)
4. Compute $\widehat{\text{fdr}}(z) = \hat{\pi}_0 \hat{f}_0(z) / \hat{f}(z)$

. . .

The R package `locfdr` automates this.

---

## When Does This Work?

### Requirements for empirical Bayes FDR

1. **Large $m$**: Need thousands of tests to estimate $f(z)$ well
2. **Exchangeability**: Tests should be "comparable" (same type of measurement, similar noise structure)
3. **Mixture model is reasonable**: The two-groups framework is a good approximation

. . .

### When it can fail

- Small $m$ (say $< 100$): not enough data to estimate the mixture
- Strong correlation structure that distorts the null distribution
- Heterogeneous tests (mixing gene expression with methylation, for example)

## Summary

1. **BH procedure**: Simple; controls FDR under independence or PRDS. The default tool for high-dimensional inference.
2. **Storey's $q$-value**: Adaptive FDR control using $\hat{\pi}_0$. More powerful when most tests are null.
3. **Efron's two-groups model**: The foundational framework. The mixture $f = \pi_0 f_0 + (1-\pi_0)f_1$ unifies the procedures above.
4. **Local FDR**: A test-specific posterior probability of being null — the most informative single summary at the level of individual tests.
5. **Empirical null**: Always assess whether $f_0 = N(0,1)$ is appropriate; estimate it from the data when it is not.
