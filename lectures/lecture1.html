Lecture 1: The Gene That Wasn’t There

How False Positives Survive — and How a Histogram Catches Them

Published

Invalid Date

The serotonin gene that wasn’t

In 2003, Avshalom Caspi and colleagues reported in Science one of the most influential findings in modern psychiatric genetics. A variant in the serotonin transporter gene — 5-HTTLPR — appeared to predict which individuals exposed to stressful life events would go on to develop depression. The hypothesis was elegant: carriers of a low-expression allele produce less serotonin transporter protein and are, in consequence, more vulnerable to stress-induced depression. Nature and nurture, mediated by a single locus.

The paper accumulated more than 8,000 citations.1 It launched a subfield. Hundreds of laboratories attempted to replicate or extend the finding; graduate students built dissertations on it; textbooks held it up as a paradigm of gene-environment interaction. Anyone who took a psychology or genetics course between 2005 and 2015 likely encountered it.

In 2019, Richard Border and colleagues tested the 5-HTTLPR hypothesis — together with seventeen other “candidate gene” hypotheses for depression — in samples ranging from 62,000 to 443,000 individuals. Their analysis plan was preregistered and their methodology mirrored that of the original studies. The only material difference was sample size.

They found nothing. Not a diminished effect, but no detectable effect at all. The serotonin transporter variant bore no measurable relationship to depression, with or without stress. The same was true of the other seventeen candidates.

How does a false positive survive for fifteen years?

The short answer is that it was not one false positive but thousands, distributed across hundreds of studies in a field that ran many statistical tests without accounting for how many tests it was running. The remedy — procedures for controlling the rate of false discoveries when many hypotheses are tested in parallel — had been available the entire time. It simply was not in use.

This lecture concerns that general problem rather than the serotonin gene specifically: what happens when many hypotheses are tested, and what the resulting evidence looks like when the multiplicity is ignored. The remainder of the workshop builds the diagnostic and inferential tools to catch the failure mode that produced 5-HTTLPR.

Three thousand and fifty-one tests

Consider a concrete dataset. In 1999, Todd Golub and colleagues published a landmark paper in Science asking whether two leukemias — acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) and acute myeloid leukemia (AML) — could be distinguished by gene expression alone. They profiled 72 patients on Affymetrix microarrays, measuring thousands of transcripts in parallel. After preprocessing, the dataset retains 3,051 genes per patient. The Golub dataset will recur throughout the workshop.

The 72 patients comprise 47 ALL and 25 AML cases. The two diseases differ in prognosis, treatment response, and underlying biology. The natural question is which genes are differentially expressed between the groups.

The motivations are clear. A clinician might seek diagnostic markers, a biologist a molecular account of the distinction, a computational researcher a basis for classification.

The standard procedure is a two-sample test on each gene in turn: compute a test statistic, derive a p-value, and judge whether the difference reaches a chosen significance threshold.

Performed across all 3,051 genes, this yields 3,051 p-values.

The implications of that number are routinely underappreciated. A p-value of 0.05 means that if a particular gene is not differentially expressed — if no real difference exists between ALL and AML — then the probability of obtaining a test statistic at least as extreme as the observed one, by chance alone, is 5 percent. For a single test, a 5 percent error rate is a tolerable cost.

But the test is not performed once. It is performed 3,051 times.

Suppose, counterfactually, that no gene were truly differentially expressed. One would still expect roughly 5 percent of the tests to attain significance at the 0.05 level by chance. Five percent of 3,051 is approximately 153. The investigator would obtain 153 “significant” results from pure noise.

The false positive rate remains 5 percent; the false positive count is 153. The biologist examining the output does not perceive a rate. She perceives a list of genes — long enough to look like a discovery.

This is the multiple testing problem. It is not subtle, and the underlying mathematics belongs to a first course in probability. Nevertheless it deceived a field of psychiatric genetics for fifteen years and continues to claim victims in every discipline that tests many hypotheses against a single dataset. By contemporary standards, 3,051 genes is a small experiment: routine RNA-seq experiments now measure on the order of 20,000.

The structure of the problem becomes clear in a single picture.

A first diagnostic: the p-value histogram

The single most informative display in high-dimensional inference is neither a volcano plot nor a heatmap. It is a histogram of p-values.

Plot the 3,051 p-values from the Golub dataset across the unit interval, with the bin counts on the vertical axis.

Were none of the genes differentially expressed, the histogram would be flat. Under the null hypothesis, p-values are uniformly distributed on \([0,1]\) — not asymptotically but exactly.2 Equal counts in every bin are precisely what no signal looks like.

The Golub histogram is not flat. It exhibits a pronounced spike near zero — several hundred genes with very small p-values — followed by a roughly flat region across the remainder of the interval. The spike represents genuinely differentially expressed genes; the flat region represents null genes whose p-values follow the expected uniform distribution.

This single plot conveys three pieces of information.

Whether signal exists. A flat histogram reflects no detectable structure; a spike near zero indicates that something is present.

Roughly how much signal exists. The height of the flat region gauges the density of null p-values. If the flat region accounts for 80 percent of the total mass, then approximately 80 percent of the genes are null and 20 percent carry signal. The Golub spike is large — consonant with the substantial transcriptomic differences between ALL and AML.

Whether the analysis is trustworthy. A well-specified analysis produces a flat right tail and a left-side spike. Departures from this shape are diagnostic. Bins that rise toward the right indicate a conservative test; a bump in the middle indicates a misspecified test statistic; a leftward shift with no flat region indicates that the null model itself is wrong. Such pathologies are common, and each is a signal to repair the analysis before interpreting any results.

The discipline this imposes is straightforward: examine the histogram before consulting gene lists, adjusted p-values, or correction procedures. The plot takes ten seconds to produce and indicates whether the rest of the analysis is worth performing.

The histogram returns in Lecture 2, where it serves not merely as a diagnostic but as an estimator. The height of the flat region encodes not only the presence of null genes but their proportion, and that proportion is the central quantity in false discovery rate estimation.

Bonferroni and family-wise error rate control

Suppose, then, that the histogram exhibits clear signal and one wishes to declare some genes significant. At the conventional 0.05 threshold, the analysis will admit on the order of one hundred false positives. How should the threshold be adjusted?

The oldest answer is the Bonferroni correction: divide the significance level by the number of tests. In place of the threshold \(p < 0.05\), one uses \(p < 0.05/3051 \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-5}\).

The reasoning is direct. Let the probability of any false positive — the family-wise error rate (FWER) — be bounded at \(\alpha\). If each of \(m\) tests is conducted at level \(\alpha/m\), the union bound guarantees that the probability of at least one false positive across all \(m\) tests is at most \(m \cdot (\alpha/m) = \alpha\).

The Bonferroni procedure has three virtues: it is conceptually clean, easy to communicate, and assumption-free with respect to the dependence structure among tests. It applies whether the tests are independent or correlated, and whether the number of tests is ten or ten million. The genome-wide association literature, with on the order of a million genetic variants per study, has standardized on a significance threshold of \(5 \times 10^{-8}\), which is essentially Bonferroni at the genome-wide scale.3

Bonferroni does what it promises. Applied to 3,051 tests at \(\alpha = 0.05\), it guarantees that the probability of any false positive among the declared discoveries is at most 5 percent.

The difficulty is that this is the wrong guarantee.

Consider what the FWER actually controls: the probability of any false positive — even one. In a list of 500 declared significant genes, Bonferroni assures that not a single discovery is false. The threshold required to provide that assurance is correspondingly stringent, and the cost is the loss of most of the real signal.

For some inferential problems, the FWER is precisely the right criterion. An astronomer announcing the detection of gravitational waves should require very strong protection against any false discovery; the discipline accordingly adopted a “five-sigma” standard, corresponding to a per-test error rate near \(1/3{,}500{,}000\). The cost of a false announcement is large.

For a biologist screening thousands of genes to nominate candidates for follow-up experimentation, the inferential goal is different. The aim is not to guarantee that every gene on the list is real but to produce a useful list, in which most discoveries are genuine and the false-positive contamination is acceptably small. A list of 500 genes containing 25 false positives — a 5 percent rate of false discoveries — is, for most exploratory applications, fully serviceable; subsequent validation experiments will adjudicate the individual claims.

Bonferroni cannot produce such a list because it is engineered to control a different error rate, and in service of that goal it discards most of the real signal.

The required reorientation is from “did I make any false positives?” to “what fraction of my declared discoveries are false?” The latter question has a quantitative answer, and Lecture 2 develops it. The remainder of the present lecture demonstrates what happens when no correction is applied at all.

The dead salmon

In 2009, the neuroscientist Craig Bennett and colleagues placed a dead Atlantic salmon in an fMRI scanner.4

The salmon was presented with a series of photographs depicting humans in social situations, and the standard fMRI analysis pipeline was applied — ostensibly to determine the emotional valence of each image. The scanner measured blood-oxygenation signals across thousands of small volume elements (voxels), and a statistical test was applied at each voxel to assess whether its signal varied with stimulus condition.

Without correction for multiple comparisons, a cluster of voxels in the salmon’s brain cavity exhibited statistically significant “neural activity.” The dead fish appeared to be processing human emotion.

With standard multiple-comparisons correction, the apparent signal disappeared, as it had to: the salmon was dead.

Bennett’s conference poster on the experiment circulated widely, both for its memorable framing and for its substantive point. Every fMRI analysis involves thousands of voxels, each tested independently; the inferential problem is structurally identical to the one in genomics. Bennett’s accompanying survey of the fMRI literature found that 25 to 40 percent of published studies failed to report any correction for multiple comparisons. The poster was memorable; the survey was a serious indictment.

Salmon and gene present the same problem in different domains. The Golub experiment yields 3,051 tests across genes; an fMRI study yields tens of thousands across voxels. The expression levels become blood-oxygenation contrasts, but the inferential structure is identical: many tests at a 5 percent per-test error rate, and a sufficient quantity of tests to render false positives inevitable.

The salmon is a useful illustration because it makes the multiple-testing problem concrete in a way that abstract calculations do not. The salmon, however, was a deliberate demonstration and was caught immediately. The 5-HTTLPR literature is the corresponding case in which the false positives were not caught for fifteen years.

A field-wide false positive

Return to the serotonin gene.

Caspi et al. (2003) reported the original finding. Over the subsequent fifteen years, hundreds of studies attempted to replicate or extend it. Some confirmed the original result, others reported the opposite direction, and many reported no effect. Meta-analyses, which aggregate evidence across studies, themselves disagreed depending on inclusion criteria and weighting.

The pattern should have been recognized as a warning sign. When an effect is real, replications converge on its direction and approximate magnitude. When studies disagree on whether an effect is positive, negative, or absent, the most parsimonious interpretation is that what is being measured is noise.

The field nevertheless had reasons to persist. The biology was plausible: serotonin is demonstrably involved in mood regulation. The original study was well-designed by the standards of its era. And a sufficient number of positive replications appeared in the literature to sustain belief, particularly under favorable readings of the meta-analytic evidence.

In 2019, Border et al. settled the question. Eighteen candidate-gene hypotheses for depression, including 5-HTTLPR, were tested in samples ranging from tens to hundreds of thousands of individuals, with preregistered analysis plans and consistent methodology. None of the eighteen replicated.

In parallel, a different approach to the genetics of depression had been yielding genuine results. Genome-wide association studies (GWAS), which test on the order of a million variants and apply the stringent \(5 \times 10^{-8}\) threshold, had identified more than 100 genetic loci associated with depression by 2019. The loci that emerged from properly corrected analyses bore essentially no relation to those identified by the uncorrected candidate-gene literature. Two approaches studying the same biology arrived at completely different answers, and the source of the divergence was the multiple-testing correction.

Three reinforcing mechanisms allowed the false positives to persist.

Publication bias. Significant results for 5-HTTLPR were preferentially published; null results disproportionately remained unpublished. The visible literature was a biased sample of the actual evidence.

Winner’s curse. Among small, noisy studies, those that crossed the significance threshold necessarily overestimated the underlying effect: only inflated estimates produced significant test statistics in underpowered samples. Early effect-size estimates were therefore systematically too large, and subsequent better-calibrated studies appeared as “failures to replicate” rather than as more accurate measurements.

The garden of forking paths. Any analysis admits many defensible choices: which covariates to include, which subgroups to examine, which outcome measure to adopt, how to operationalize “stressful life events.” Each choice is a branch point, and analysts could explore alternatives — without any intent to deceive — until a significant result emerged. This is not equivalent to running thousands of explicit tests, but it has the same statistical consequence: the effective number of tests far exceeds the number reported.

None of this requires malfeasance. The researchers studying 5-HTTLPR were, by and large, careful scientists who believed in their hypothesis and interpreted ambiguous evidence in its favor. That is not fraud; it is ordinary human cognition. The failure was not in the individual scientists but in the statistical framework, which did not account for the scale of testing actually being performed.

The remedy had been available throughout. GWAS adopted it from the outset: test all variants, correct for all of them, and accept the stringent threshold. The candidate-gene literature tested only a handful of variants per study, which appeared to obviate correction — but across hundreds of studies, each making dozens of analytical choices, the effective number of tests was enormous. The correction should have been applied not study-by-study but at the level of the literature.

The 5-HTTLPR episode is not a story about poor scientists. It is a story about what occurs when a field’s statistical framework is not commensurate with the scale of its inquiry. The framework adequate to that scale exists, and Lecture 2 develops it. The accompanying lab applies the procedures of this lecture to the Golub dataset directly: 3,051 tests, the diagnostic histogram, the Bonferroni threshold, and an explicit comparison between “controls any error” and “supports useful inference.” That comparison is the territory occupied by the false discovery rate.
