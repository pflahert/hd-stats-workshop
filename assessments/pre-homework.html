Purpose

This pre-homework ensures everyone starts Day 1 with a shared foundation in hypothesis testing and \(p\)-values. It also introduces the multiple testing problem and includes a short exercise using AI tools.

Learning Outcomes: LO1, LO3

Part 1: Review of Hypothesis Testing (30 min)

Reading

Review the basics of hypothesis testing from your introductory statistics course or one of these free references:

  • OpenIntro Statistics, Ch. 5: openintro.org
  • An Introduction to Statistical Learning, Ch. 13 (Multiple Testing): statlearning.com

Questions

Q1.1: In your own words, define: (a) Null hypothesis (\(H_0\)), (b) Alternative hypothesis (\(H_1\)), (c) \(p\)-value, (d) Type I error, (e) Type II error.

Q1.2: A researcher tests whether a new drug reduces blood pressure. They report \(p = 0.03\).

  1. State the null and alternative hypotheses.
  2. At significance level \(\alpha = 0.05\), do they reject \(H_0\)?
  3. The \(p\)-value is NOT the probability that \(H_0\) is true. Explain why not, in one or two sentences.

Q1.3: If you perform a test at \(\alpha = 0.05\) and the null hypothesis is actually true, what is the probability of a Type I error? If you perform 20 such independent tests (all with true nulls), what is the probability of getting at least one Type I error?

Hint: \(P(\text{at least one}) = 1 - P(\text{none}) = 1 - (1 - 0.05)^{20}\).

Part 2: The Multiple Testing Problem (20 min)

A thought experiment

Imagine you are a genomics researcher. You have gene expression data for 10,000 genes measured on 40 patients (20 with disease, 20 healthy controls). You run a \(t\)-test for each gene to see if its expression differs between the two groups.

Q2.1: If none of the 10,000 genes are truly associated with the disease, how many genes would you expect to have \(p < 0.05\) by chance alone?

Q2.2: Now suppose 100 of the 10,000 genes are truly associated. The remaining 9,900 null genes still produce approximately \(9{,}900 \times 0.05 \approx 495\) false positives, plus some additional true positives. If you report all genes with \(p < 0.05\) as “significant,” roughly what fraction of your reported genes would be false positives?

Q2.3: Suggest at least one approach to deal with this problem. You don’t need to know the formal name—just describe the idea intuitively.
